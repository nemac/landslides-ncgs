Data Description

The North Carolina Geological Survey’s Landslide Hazard Mapping Program helps people across western North Carolina understand where landslides have happened in the past, where they are more likely to occur, and which communities, roads, and natural areas could be most at risk. This information supports safer planning, stronger communities, and better preparedness during emergencies.

Below you’ll find descriptions of the Landslide Inventory and Landslide Susceptibility map layers. These layers give residents, planners, and emergency responders an easy way to explore past landslide activity and make informed decisions that support long-term resilience in western North Carolina.

The Landslide Features layer shows the observed extent of mapped landslides. When possible, each landslide is divided into three parts: the initial slide area (Source Area), the zone where material moves downhill (Transport Zone), and the area where the material comes to rest (Deposit).

Observation Points

Observation Points mark locations where landslides have affected roads or structures, as well as places where photos or notes about the landslide have been collected.

Ground Ruptures

Ground Ruptures are shown as lines that represent cracks and breaks in the ground. These features often appear before a landslide and can serve as early warning signs.

Potential Channelized Debris Flow Pathways

The Potential Debris Flow Pathways layer highlights areas where debris from a landslide could travel if a slope failure occurs in a high susceptibility area. Because landscapes can change over time through development, natural processes, or future landslides, the exact paths of debris flows may also change. For this reason, the layer represents broad areas that could potentially be affected by future landslide impacts, rather than precise predicted paths.

The Probability of Sliding layer shows areas where debris flows and debris slides could begin on natural slopes — meaning hillsides that haven’t been reshaped or altered by people. Landslides don’t happen often and require specific conditions, but they become more likely in western North Carolina when the region receives 5 inches or more of rain in 24 hours. This rainfall threshold is the basis for the Probability of Sliding layer. If you plan to build on or modify slopes labeled as High or Moderate Probability of Sliding, use extra caution, these areas can be unstable even without heavy rainfall.
